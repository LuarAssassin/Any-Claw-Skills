# Productivity Domain Knowledge

## Getting Things Done (GTD)

The GTD methodology by David Allen organizes work into five stages:

1. **Capture** -- Collect every task, idea, and commitment into a trusted system.
2. **Clarify** -- Process each item: Is it actionable? If yes, define the next action. If no, trash it, incubate it, or file it as reference.
3. **Organize** -- Place items into categories:
   - **Next Actions**: tasks to do as soon as possible.
   - **Projects**: outcomes requiring more than one action step.
   - **Waiting For**: items delegated or blocked.
   - **Someday/Maybe**: ideas to revisit later.
   - **Calendar**: date-specific commitments.
4. **Reflect** -- Review lists weekly. Update priorities, remove completed items, re-evaluate deferred work.
5. **Engage** -- Choose what to work on based on context, time available, energy, and priority.

The **two-minute rule**: if a task takes less than two minutes, do it immediately rather than tracking it.

## Eisenhower Matrix

Categorize tasks along two axes -- urgency and importance:

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | **Do First** -- Crises, deadlines, critical bugs | **Schedule** -- Strategic planning, learning, relationship building |
| **Not Important** | **Delegate** -- Most emails, some meetings, interruptions | **Eliminate** -- Time-wasters, trivial busywork, excessive notifications |

Guidelines for applying the matrix:

- Default new tasks to "Schedule" unless there is a hard deadline within 48 hours.
- Regularly audit "Do First" -- if everything is urgent, nothing is prioritized.
- Move recurring "Delegate" items into automation or templates.
- Be aggressive about "Eliminate" -- protecting time is a productivity multiplier.

## Time Blocking

Time blocking allocates fixed periods on the calendar to specific types of work:

- **Deep Work Blocks** (90-120 min): creative, analytical, or coding tasks requiring uninterrupted focus. No meetings or notifications.
- **Shallow Work Blocks** (30-60 min): email, Slack, administrative tasks, quick replies.
- **Meeting Blocks**: cluster meetings together to avoid fragmenting the day.
- **Buffer Blocks** (15-30 min): padding between blocks for overflow, breaks, and context switching.

Best practices:

- Protect at least one deep work block per day, ideally in the morning.
- Batch similar activities (all emails at 10am and 4pm, not throughout the day).
- Time-box open-ended tasks to prevent them from expanding indefinitely.
- Review and adjust blocks weekly based on actual time usage.

## Pomodoro Technique

A time management method using fixed intervals:

1. Choose a task.
2. Set a timer for **25 minutes** (one "pomodoro").
3. Work with full focus until the timer rings.
4. Take a **5-minute break**.
5. After every 4 pomodoros, take a **15-30 minute break**.

Adapt the interval length to fit the work: 50/10 for deep programming, 25/5 for varied tasks.

## Task Prioritization Frameworks

### MoSCoW Method
- **Must Have**: non-negotiable deliverables.
- **Should Have**: important but not fatal if deferred.
- **Could Have**: nice-to-have enhancements.
- **Won't Have (this time)**: explicitly out of scope.

### RICE Scoring
Score = (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort

- **Reach**: how many people/tasks are affected.
- **Impact**: degree of improvement (0.25 = minimal, 3 = massive).
- **Confidence**: percentage certainty in estimates.
- **Effort**: person-months or hours required.

## Email Management

- **Inbox Zero**: process every email to a decision -- reply, delegate, defer, archive, or delete.
- **Touch It Once**: avoid reading an email and leaving it unactioned.
- **Batch processing**: check email 2-3 times per day at set intervals rather than continuously.
- **Templates and canned responses**: create reusable replies for frequently asked questions.
- **Filters and rules**: auto-sort newsletters, notifications, and CC-only threads into dedicated folders.

## Weekly Review Checklist

1. Clear all inboxes (email, chat, physical).
2. Review completed tasks and celebrate progress.
3. Update project statuses and next actions.
4. Review calendar for the upcoming week.
5. Identify top 3 priorities for the coming week.
6. Check "Waiting For" items and follow up if stale.
7. Review "Someday/Maybe" for anything now relevant.
8. Adjust time blocks based on upcoming deadlines.
